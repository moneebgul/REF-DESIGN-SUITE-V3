What are we designing?

Optional design targets (0 = none). The Results page and PDF report compare the achieved net volume against these.
Energy + EEI shows the EU class. Cooling design hides the rating and focuses on capacity & run-time.
Serial / single-evaporator: one loop, one common Te set by the coldest compartment (the freezer) — warmer compartments are cooled at that same cold Te, so their effective approach is large (the serial penalty). Parallel / dual-loop: each compartment cooled at its own Te with an independent run-time.

Target is the cycling set-point. Approach is how much colder the coil runs than the compartment, so Te = target − approach (shown, drives the compressor map). In serial / single-evaporator mode every compartment shares the coldest Te, so warmer compartments show their large effective approach (Δeff) and their own approach input is greyed — only the coldest compartment's approach sets the loop.

Edge & gasket
Extra edge/flange conduction beyond 1-D walls. Anchored to ACRC TR-21 (18 ft³ top-mount): ~0.17 base (steel flanges + door seal, no anti-sweat). The anti-sweat condenser loop below is modelled separately, so leave this at the base and don't also inflate it for the loop. Calibrate against a measured cabinet UA when available.
Edge losses run ~30% of total cabinet load (CFD/RHL studies); the explicit gasket carries part, the uplift the rest. Calibrate to a reverse-heat-leak test.

In-system correction (datasheet → your cabinet)
The supplier map is rated at a calorimeter standard (fixed return-gas, subcooling, no line ΔP). Your system delivers less at the same Te/Tc. These factors scale the interpolated capacity and power to in-system values (COP follows automatically). Calibrate from a reference point: capacity factor = measured cap ÷ map cap; power factor = measured power ÷ map power. Leave at 1.00 for raw rated-map behaviour.
Fans
Internal circulation fans (optional — static cabinets)
A circulation fan forces the compartment's inner surfaces from natural to forced convection. Captured automatically: (1) fan power added to energy; (2) forced inner film on that compartment's walls — note this slightly raises wall heat gain, since the cold wall couples to the air better. The cooling-performance benefit is on the evaporator side: forced air improves air-to-evaporator coupling, so set a smaller evaporator approach for that compartment (higher Te, better COP) — ideally from the coil tool. It also suppresses shelf condensation. Net energy rises or falls depending on the approach gain vs the fan power.
